Create-board · radical directions

Four directions that throw out the "title input + template picker" skeleton. Each one reframes what creating a board even is — a tiny board you build, a sentence you write, an object on a canvas, or just a single line in a spotlight bar.

G · Live Board Sketch H · Mad-lib I · Spatial Constellation J · One-line Ambient
DIRECTION · G

Live Board Sketch

Modal opens with an actual tiny kanban inside. The columns are real, named in place — you Tab through them or click "+ Column" to add. Templates collapse into a single ✨ "Suggest columns" button next to the title that pre-fills them. The creator is the board, not a form about one.

DIRECTION · H

Mad-lib

A single sentence in big serif. "Track ___ from ___ to ___." Fill in the verbs, the board materializes below. The middle "stops" can be 2-5; click the dashed "+ stop" pill to add another. Feels like writing, not configuring. The board it generates lives below as a tiny live preview that updates with every keystroke.

DIRECTION · I

Spatial Constellation

Your existing boards float as labeled "pucks" on a soft dark canvas — a constellation. A new untitled puck pulses lime in the center; type its name into the floating pill, pick its shape from the small dropdown, hit Enter, it joins the constellation. Treats boards as objects in a place, not entries in a list.

One-line Ambient

Press B (or click +) anywhere — a single floating bar drops down ~120px from the top, like a Spotlight or ⌘K palette. One large input. A small "shape" pill on the right (click to swap between 6 templates). Hit Enter, you're in the new board. The most opinionated answer to "what's the minimum this needs to be?"

How these compare

G trades visual lightness for genuine usefulness — you actually configure the board, no separate "edit columns" step later. H is the most charming on first encounter but gets twee fast and can't express forking flows. I is the most ambitious — it doesn't just redesign the modal, it redesigns the boards page itself, which is a much bigger commitment. J is the closest to a "we trust the user, just let them work" answer — almost anti-design, very pro-velocity.
